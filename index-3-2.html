A Journey Through Black Art

This website explores the transformative works of African American artists across three pivotal eras: the Enslavement Era, the Harlem Renaissance, and the Black Arts Movement, highlighting their cultural significance and resistance against oppression.

Enslavement Era (1619–1865): Functional Art as Resistance

During the Enslavement Era, African American visual art emerged under oppressive conditions where enslaved individuals were denied formal artistic training. Artworks like pottery and quilts served functional purposes while embedding cultural memory and resistance. The transatlantic slave trade brought African aesthetic traditions, while plantation life necessitated covert expressions of identity through found materials like clay and fabric scraps.

David Drake's Storage Jar (1857)

An enslaved potter in South Carolina, Drake inscribed poetry on his stoneware vessels, like "I made this jar for cash / Though it's called lucre trash," defying literacy bans and showcasing subtle rebellion.

Harriet Powers' Bible Quilt (1895-1898)

Born enslaved in Georgia, Powers created narrative quilts post-emancipation, depicting biblical and astronomical scenes, preserving African storytelling traditions.

Critical Perspectives

  • Cultural Perspective: These artworks preserved African aesthetic traditions, like call-and-response in Drake's inscriptions and West African textile patterns in Powers' quilts, maintaining cultural continuity despite forced assimilation.
  • Racial Perspective: Art was a form of resistance against racial oppression, as enslaved artists like Drake risked punishment to assert literacy and humanity, challenging dehumanizing narratives of slavery.

Harlem Renaissance (1918–1937): A Cultural Awakening

The Harlem Renaissance marked a cultural awakening where African American artists embraced modernism and racial pride. The Great Migration and the New Negro Movement, led by figures like Alain Locke, drove artists to depict Black life with dignity, influenced by jazz and urban culture.

Aaron Douglas' Aspects of Negro Life (1934)

A leading painter, Douglas created murals blending African motifs with modernist silhouettes to depict Black history and resilience.

Jacob Lawrence's The Migration Series (1940-41)

A 60-panel series depicting the Great Migration, using vibrant colors and narrative forms to highlight Black mobility and resilience.

Critical Perspectives

  • Class Perspective: Artworks like Lawrence's The Migration Series highlighted the struggles of the Black working class, fostering solidarity in a society stratified by wealth and race.
  • Cultural Perspective: Douglas's African-inspired murals celebrated Black heritage, aligning with the Harlem Renaissance's push for cultural reclamation and self-definition.

Black Arts Movement (1965–1975): Art as Activism

Tied to the Civil Rights and Black Power movements, the Black Arts Movement positioned art as a tool for political activism and Black identity. Events like the assassination of Malcolm X and urban uprisings spurred artists to reject assimilationist aesthetics for bold, Afrocentric expressions.

Betye Saar's The Liberation of Aunt Jemima (1972)

A mixed-media assemblage reclaiming the mammy stereotype, arming the figure with a rifle to symbolize empowerment.

David Hammons' Injustice Case (1970)

A body print and mixed-media work critiquing the judicial system's treatment of Black activists, using satire to challenge racial injustice.

Critical Perspectives

  • Racial Perspective: Saar and Hammons used art to dismantle racist stereotypes and depict Black agency, aligning with Black Power's call for self-determination.
  • Cultural Perspective: Their works integrated African American vernacular culture, like Saar's use of found objects, to assert a distinct cultural identity.
